Life in the Field

For students new to field work, the experience can be as challenging as it is rewarding. Preparation is key to safety and success. Below are consolidated references and guides for maintaining health, safety, and morale in various extreme environments.

Jump to: General Field Life | Life at Sea | Life in the Cold | Life on Mountains | Life in the Heat |

General Field Life

Field work requires rigorous planning, risk assessment, and team communication. Key principles include:

  • Risk Assessment: Always identify potential hazards (terrain, wildlife, weather) before deploying.
  • Communication: Establish clear check-in protocols and carry appropriate comms gear (satellite phone, inReach) for remote areas.
  • The "Buddy System": Never work alone in hazardous environments.
  • Mental Resilience: Field work can be isolating. Maintain a positive team dynamic and set daily routines.

Life in the Cold

Surviving and working in polar or sub-zero environments depends on proper layering and managing moisture. Sweat is your enemy.

  • Layering: Base layer (wicking, no cotton), mid-layer (insulation: fleece/down), shell (wind/waterproof).
  • Extremities: Protect head, hands, and feet. Mittens are warmer than gloves.
  • Calories: Body heat generation requires significant caloric intake (often 4000-6000 kcal/day).

Life on Mountains

High altitude work introduces hypoxia (low oxygen) risks alongside variable weather.

  • Acclimatization: "Climb high, sleep low." Ascend gradually to allow your body to adjust.
  • Hydration: Altitude increases water loss through respiration. Drink 3-4 liters daily.
  • Sun Protection: UV radiation increases by ~4% for every 1000ft gain. Wear Category 3 or 4 sunglasses.

Life in the Heat

Desert fieldwork is a battle against dehydration and heat stress.

  • Hydration: Drink before you are thirsty. Replenish electrolytes, not just water.
  • Timing: Schedule strenuous work for early morning or late afternoon (avoid 10 AM - 4 PM).
  • Clothing: Loose-fitting, light-colored clothing reflects sun and allows airflow. Wide-brimmed hats are essential.
