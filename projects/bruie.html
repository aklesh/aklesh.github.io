Buoyant Rover for Under-Ice Exploration (BRUIE)

BRUIE is a robotic submersible developed at NASA’s Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) designed to explore the unknown worlds beneath the ice. Unlike traditional submersibles, BRUIE is buoyant—it floats against the underside of the ice crust and drives along it using two independent wheels.

Design for Ocean Worlds

The search for life elsewhere in the solar system is increasingly focused on “Ocean Worlds” like Jupiter’s moon Europa and Saturn’s moon Enceladus, which mask vast liquid oceans beneath thick icy shells. BRUIE’s unique design addresses the specific challenges of these environments:

  • Ice-Water Interface: Life on Earth is often concentrated at interfaces. By anchoring to the ice from fewer, BRUIE can stay in the most scientifically rich zone without constantly fighting currents.
  • Power Efficiency: BRUIE can “sleep” against the ice for weeks or months, waking up only to take measurements. This low-power mode is critical for long-duration missions where solar power is unavailable.
  • Mobility: Its two wide, toothed wheels allow it to traverse rough under-ice topography while sensing the ocean chemistry below.

Global Expeditions

To prove the technology is ready for deep space, the BRUIE team has tested the rover in some of the most extreme environments on Earth.

Alaska (Arctic)

Starting in 2012, prototype versions of BRUIE were deployed in frozen lakes near Utqiaġvik (Barrow), Alaska. These early tests validated the mechanical concept and the rover’s ability to remain anchored against the ice while tethered. The team demonstrated that the rover could effectively image the ice-water interface and gather temperature and salinity profiles.

Antarctica (Casey Station)

In 2019, the team took the latest iteration of BRUIE to East Antarctica’s Casey Station. This marked the first endurance test in the Southern Hemisphere, simulating the conditions of a mission to Europa. * Drilling Through: The team had to drill through meters of solid sea ice to deploy the rover. * Autonomy: Tests focused on the rover’s ability to autonomously navigate and hold station without constant human interaction.

Deployment of BRUIE in Antarctica. Video: NASA/JPL-Caltech

Impact

BRUIE represents a new architecture for planetary exploration—the “crawler” that bridges the gap between a stationary lander and a free-swimming submersible. The data gathered from Earth’s polar regions not only advances our readiness for Europa but also provides immediate value to climate scientists studying the interaction between our own oceans and ice sheets.
