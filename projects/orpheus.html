Orpheus: Exploring the Hadal Zone

Orpheus is a new class of autonomous underwater vehicle (AUV) developed through a collaboration between WHOI and NASA’s Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL). Designed to explore the ocean’s deepest frontiers—the Hadal zone (6,000 to 11,000 meters)—Orpheus serves as a technology pathfinder for future missions to ocean worlds like Europa.

Mission & Design

Current deep-ocean exploration relies on massive, expensive vehicles. Orpheus breaks this paradigm by being small, modular, and relatively low-cost, allowing for “swarms” of robots to collectively map and explore vast trench systems.

  • Visual-Inertial Odometry (xVIO): Adapted from NASA’s Mars Helicopter Ingenuity, Orpheus uses visual feature tracking to navigate without GPS in the pitch-black depths. By creating 3D maps of the seafloor in real-time, it can identify scientific targets and navigate autonomously.
  • Hadal Rated: Capable of withstanding pressures exceeding 1,000 atmospheres.
  • Modular Payload: Can carry eDNA samplers, cameras, and chemical sensors to hunt for life in the deep trenches.

Orpheus AUV exploration concepts. Video: WHOI/JPL

Expeditions

2021 NOAA Okeanos Explorer

A critical technology demonstration mission off the U.S. East Coast. This expedition tested the terrain-relative navigation (TRN) system in shallower waters, validating the vision-based navigation software that would later guide it in the trenches.

2019 R/V Neil Armstrong

Early field testing of the prototype vehicle, focusing on launch/recovery operations and basic system functionality.

Impact

Orpheus is not just about Earth’s oceans. It validates the “autonomy architecture” needed for a mission to Europa. Because radio signals cannot penetrate ice or water, a robot exploring an alien ocean must be smart enough to navigate, avoid hazards, and select science targets entirely on its own—Orpheus is finding the way.
