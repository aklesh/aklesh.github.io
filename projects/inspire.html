INSPIRE

INSPIRE (Interplanetary NanoSpacecraft Pathfinder In Relevant Environment) was a pioneering NASA/JPL project designed to be the world’s first CubeSat mission to operate in deep space, beyond Earth’s orbit.

Mission Objective

The primary goal of INSPIRE was to demonstrate that the CubeSat form factor—low-cost, standardized nanosatellites—could survive and function in the harsh environment of interplanetary space. It served as a critical technology pathfinder for subsequent missions like MarCO.

Key Technologies

INSPIRE was the testbed for several technologies that became standard for deep-space smallsats: * Iris Radio: Validating the X-band transponder for communication with the Deep Space Network (DSN) from millions of kilometers away. * C&DH (Command & Data Handling): Testing robust computing architectures capable of surviving radiation events. * Navigation: Demonstrating radiometric ranging and Doppler tracking for orbit determination.

The Precursor to MarCO

Before the twin MarCO spacecraft could relay InSight's landing data from Mars, the fundamental question "Can a CubeSat survive deep space?" had to be answered. INSPIRE was designed to answer that question, proving out the radio, power, and thermal designs that would later be flown to Mars.

Legacy

While the flight units faced challenges, the legacy of INSPIRE is cemented in the success of the missions that followed. The hardware, software, and operational procedures developed for INSPIRE were directly inherited by MarCO, Artemis I CubeSats, and other deep-space explorers. It opened the door for universities and small organizations to propose missions not just to Low Earth Orbit, but to the Moon, asteroids, and beyond.
