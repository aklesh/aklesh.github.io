RAX: Radio Aurora Explorer

RAX was the first CubeSat mission sponsored by the National Science Foundation (NSF). Developed as a collaboration between the University of Michigan and SRI International, RAX demonstrated that nanosatellites could perform high-value scientific research, paving the way for the explosion of CubeSats in academia and industry.

Scientific Mission

The primary goal of RAX was to study Field-Aligned Irregularities (FAI)—plasma structures in the Earth’s ionosphere that disrupt radio communications and GPS signals. * Method: RAX operated as a bistatic radar. Powerful ground-based radars (like the Poker Flat Incoherent Scatter Radar in Alaska) transmitted signals into the ionosphere. RAX, passing overhead, measured the scattered signals to create a 3D map of the plasma irregularities.

Spacecraft Evolution

RAX-1 (2010)

Launched in November 2010, RAX-1 proved the bistatic radar concept but suffered a premature solar power failure due to a design flaw in the panel manufacturing.

RAX-2 (2011)

Launched in October 2011, RAX-2 corrected the power issues of its predecessor and operated successfully for years, collecting an unprecedented dataset of ionospheric turbulence.
