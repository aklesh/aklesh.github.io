Distributed Acoustic Sensing (DAS)

Distributed Acoustic Sensing (DAS) is a revolutionary technology that transforms standard fiber-optic cables into dense arrays of thousands of seismometers. By firing laser pulses down a fiber and measuring the backscattered light, researchers can measure strain changes along the entire length of the cable with unprecedented resolution.

Working in collaboration with Prof. Zhongwen Zhan at Caltech, we have deployed DAS arrays in some of the most dynamic environments on Earth.

Deployments

Kilauea Volcano, Hawaii

Following the massive 2018 eruption, a 3,000-channel DAS array was deployed along the caldera rim. This array provided a high-resolution “CT scan” of the subsurface magma plumbing system, revealing how the volcano breathes and deforms in response to pressure changes.

Iceland

In collaboration with Icelandic partners, DAS arrays have been deployed across volcanic and geothermal fields. The technology has proven capable of detecting: * Volcanic Tremor: Subtle vibrations indicating magma movement. * Fault Slips: Micro-earthquakes that standard networks miss. * Magma Motion: Providing early warning signals of potential eruptions.

Ridgecrest Earthquake

Following the 2019 M7.1 earthquake in California, existing telecommunications fiber was requisitioned to act as a rapid-response seismic network. This “dark fiber” experiment detected thousands of aftershocks, mapping the complex web of cross-cutting faults that ruptured during the event.

Glacial Seismology

DAS is also revolutionizing cryoseismology. Deployments on glaciers allow us to “hear” the ice cracking, sliding, and melting. * Taku Glacier: Monitoring stick-slip events. * GLASS: Investigating Glacier Lake Outburst Floods.
