Mars Cube One (MarCO)

MarCO (Mars Cube One) was a technology demonstration mission that accompanied the InSight lander to Mars in 2018. Consisting of two identical 6U CubeSats, nicknamed MarCO-A (Wall-E) and MarCO-B (Eva), the mission marked a historic milestone as the first CubeSats to operate in deep space.

Mission Overview

The primary objective of MarCO was to demonstrate that the small, low-cost CubeSat form factor could successfully survive the journey to deep space and perform a critical communications relay function. Launching on May 5, 2018, aboard an Atlas V rocket from Vandenberg Air Force Base, the twin spacecraft separated from the Centaur upper stage shortly after launch and flew their own independent trajectory to the Red Planet.

On November 26, 2018, as InSight descended through the Martian atmosphere, the MarCO spacecraft flew by Mars at a distance of approximately 3,500 km. They successfully relayed real-time telemetry from the lander back to Earth, providing mission control at JPL with immediate confirmation of InSight’s safe touchdown.

Spacecraft Design & Subsystems

Packing full interplanetary capability into a “briefcase-sized” 6U form factor (roughly 36.6 x 24.3 x 11.8 cm) required significant miniaturization and innovation.

Communications

The heart of MarCO’s relay capability was the Iris V2 transponder. This software-defined radio provided compatibility with NASA’s Deep Space Network (DSN), enabling communication over millions of kilometers. * X-band: A deployable high-gain reflectarray antenna allowed for an 8 kbps downlink rate from Mars distance (approx. 1 AU). This flat panel unfolded to mimic the gain of a parabolic dish. * UHF: A loop antenna was used to receive the 8 kbps data stream from InSight during its Entry, Descent, and Landing (EDL).

Guidance, Navigation & Control (GN&C)

Unlike typical CubeSats that rely on magnetic torquers (which don’t work in deep space), MarCO utilized a cold-gas propulsion system utilizing R-236fa propellant. Eight thrusters provided attitude control and trajectory correction maneuvers (TCMs) during the cruise to Mars. A star tracker and sun sensors provided precise attitude determination.

Impact & Future Implications

MarCO fundamentally changed the paradigm of interplanetary exploration. By proving that “smallsats” can survive and operate beyond Earth orbit, it opened the door for a new class of low-cost, targeted science missions.

“MarCO is mostly about the future… The experimental technologies validated by MarCO… are expected to inform the design of subsequent CubeSat missions, fostering a new era of more frequent and accessible space exploration.” — Andy Klesh

The success of MarCO paved the way for upcoming missions like Artemis I CubeSats, CAPSTONE, and future concepts for smallsat explorers to Venus and asteroids.
